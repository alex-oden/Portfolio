Why your design research synthesis takes three times longer than it should

After running 22 user interviews on my last project, I learned a painful lesson about when to start tagging. A practical guide to front-loading your synthesis frameworks before the first session — and the three mistakes I keep making.

AO
Alex Oden
UX & Service Designer
May 24, 2026·12 min read
Aa

I've been doing user research professionally for around five years now. In that time I've sat through hundreds of interviews and built dozens of synthesis decks. And on every single project, I've underestimated how long synthesis would take. Every. Single. Time.

On my most recent project — a SaaS onboarding redesign — I budgeted three days for synthesis after 22 user interviews. It took nine. The decks were excellent, the insights sharp, the stakeholders thrilled. But I burned through nearly two weeks of margin I didn't have.

This essay is the post-mortem I wrote for myself. If you've ever stared at a wall of sticky notes wondering where to start, this is for you.

The mistake we all make: tagging starts after the last interview

Here's the pattern I keep falling into. I script my guide, schedule sessions, dive in. I take notes during each session. After the last interview, then I sit down to make sense of it all.

The problem: by interview 22, I've forgotten the nuance of interview 1. I end up re-listening to recordings, second-guessing my tags, and reconstructing a coherent story from fragments. That reconstruction is what takes so long.

The fix isn't doing synthesis faster. The fix is starting synthesis before you finish data collection. Tag in real time. Build your framework as you learn.

This sounds obvious. And yet I keep not doing it, because in the moment, in-the-moment tagging feels like premature structure. That instinct is exactly backwards.

Three mistakes I keep making

1. I confuse "themes" with "categories"

Themes are interpretations. Categories are buckets. Categories should be created up front and stay stable: which feature, which segment, which task. Themes emerge later and should be revised constantly.

Themes (emergent)

2. I delay defining "what counts as a finding"

What constitutes a finding worth writing up? My current rule: a finding requires evidence from at least three participants from at least two segments. Anything below that becomes a "watch item".

3. I skip the "what would change my mind?" question

For every emerging finding, what evidence would falsify it? If you can't answer that, you don't have a finding — you have a guess.

"The plural of anecdote is not data, but data is not insight either. Insight is a pattern you can defend, that survives serious attempts to disprove it."

A simple framework that works

  1. Before the first interview: define categories, set the bar for a finding, draft an initial theme list.
  2. After every interview: tag against categories in real time. Revise themes within 24 hours.
  3. At the midpoint: review themes with the team. Kill the ones with no evidence.
  4. After the last interview: challenge every finding with "what would change my mind?".
  5. Synthesis is done when: you can summarise each finding in one sentence with three+ supporting participants.

What I'd tell my past self

If I could go back five years, I'd say: your interview notes are not data. They're raw material. The data emerges when you process those notes through categorisation, theme-finding, and falsification.

Front-load the structure. Tag as you go. Set your evidence bar before you collect evidence. Your future self will thank you.
